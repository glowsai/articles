## 1. Generate 225–720 AI Images

Published RTX 4090 numbers put Flux.1 Schnell at roughly 5 seconds per 1024×1024 image at 4 steps ([ComfyUI GitHub discussion #4571](https://github.com/comfyanonymous/ComfyUI/discussions/4571), Aug 2024), SDXL at about 7 seconds at 20 steps ([Prompting Pixels GPU benchmarks](https://www.promptingpixels.com/gpu-benchmarks)), and full-precision Flux.1 Dev at 15–17 seconds. Divide 3,600 seconds by those figures and one GPU-hour yields:

- **~720 images** with Flux.1 Schnell
- **~514 images** with SDXL
- **~225 images** with Flux.1 Dev FP16

That is $0.0007–$0.0022 per image. We ran the full cost breakdown — including how this compares with image APIs at $8–$120 per 1,000 images — in [our post on generating 1,000 AI images for about $2](https://glows.ai/article/generate_1000_ai_images_for_2_dollars_en). The one-click ComfyUI image on Glows.ai boots in 30–60 seconds, and [custom ComfyUI workflows](https://glows.ai/article/run_comfyui_on_glowsai) let you queue the whole batch unattended.

## 2. Turn 6 Photos Into 5-Second Videos

The Wan team's own benchmark for [Wan 2.2](https://github.com/Wan-Video/Wan2.2), the Apache 2.0-licensed video model, puts a 5-second 720p24 clip at under 9 minutes of render time on a single RTX 4090 using the TI2V-5B variant. One hour therefore fits about six clips — roughly 30 seconds of finished 720p footage — at about $0.07 of compute each.

Thirty seconds may not sound like much until you price the alternative: hosted video generators typically run $20–$100/month with daily credit caps and watermarks on free tiers. The full walkthrough, including the built-in ComfyUI template, is in [how to turn a photo into a video with AI in 10 minutes](https://glows.ai/article/turn_photo_into_video_ai_10_minutes_en).

## 3. Push 220,000 to 1.7 Million LLM Tokens

For chat-scale models, a cheap cloud GPU is a private token factory. On an RTX 4090 running Llama 3.1 8B, [SitePoint's 2026 Ollama vs. vLLM benchmark](https://www.sitepoint.com/ollama-vs-vllm-performance-benchmark-2026/) measured about 62 tokens/s single-stream on Ollama (Q4_K_M) and 71 tokens/s on vLLM (FP16) — and, with vLLM's continuous batching serving 10 concurrent requests, roughly 485 tokens/s aggregate.

Over one hour:

- **Single stream:** ~223,000 tokens — on the order of two full novels of generated text
- **Batched serving:** ~1.7 million tokens, or about $0.28 per million tokens generated

That is enough to summarize a document backlog, run a synthetic-data job, or serve a small team's internal chatbot for an evening. Preconfigured Ollama, vLLM, and SGLang images are available; see the quick-start for [running DeepSeek-R1 on Glows.ai](https://glows.ai/article/deepseek_r1) for the pattern.

## 4. Transcribe a 20-Hour Podcast Backlog

[Tom's Hardware benchmarked Whisper transcription on 18 GPUs](https://www.tomshardware.com/news/whisper-audio-transcription-gpus-benchmarked) and clocked top consumer cards at up to ~3,000 words per minute — around 20× the pace of typical speech at 140–160 wpm. And that test used the stock OpenAI implementation: [faster-whisper](https://github.com/SYSTRAN/faster-whisper) reports consistent 4× speedups over it with the same large-v3 accuracy, and community pipelines with batching push an RTX 4090 to 70–100× real time.

Being conservative and sticking to the ~20× figure: one GPU-hour transcribes roughly **20 hours of audio** — a season of podcasts, a semester of lecture recordings, or every user interview you've been meaning to process — for $0.49, with word-level timestamps and no per-minute API fees. At 70×, the same coin buys close to three days of audio.

## 5. Synthesize 6–30 Hours of Speech

Text-to-speech on a GPU is measured in real-time factor (RTF): the ratio of compute time to audio produced. [GigaGPU's 2026 Kokoro vs. XTTS-v2 comparison](https://gigagpu.com/kokoro-vs-xtts-v2-low-latency-tts/) puts Kokoro at RTF ~0.03 (community RTX 4090 runs report 0.04–0.06) and XTTS-v2 at RTF ~0.15.

Inverted, one GPU-hour buys:

- **~16–33 hours** of Kokoro speech — enough to voice a full audiobook draft several times over
- **~6.5 hours** of XTTS-v2 output *with voice cloning*

For Mandarin speakers, Glows.ai also ships a preconfigured image for BreezyVoice, a Taiwanese-Mandarin voice cloning model — the [BreezyVoice tutorial](https://glows.ai/article/breezyvoice) takes you from a reference sample to generated speech in one session.

## 6. Train a Complete SDXL LoRA

The one that surprises people: a single GPU-hour covers an entire fine-tune. On an RTX 4090, kohya_ss users report SDXL LoRA training at 2.3–2.5 seconds per iteration with batch size 5 — about 2 images/second effective ([kohya_ss discussion #2166](https://github.com/bmaltais/kohya_ss/discussions/2166), cross-checked against [Puget Systems' consumer-GPU LoRA analysis](https://www.pugetsystems.com/labs/articles/stable-diffusion-lora-training-consumer-gpu-analysis/)). A typical character or style LoRA of 1,500–3,000 total steps therefore lands in roughly 15–45 minutes.

In other words: your own custom model — your art style, your product, your mascot — for under $0.40 of compute, with enough of the hour left to generate test images with the result. ## What One Hour Does Not Buy

A fair menu lists what's not on it:

- **Model downloads on the first run.** SDXL is ~7 GB, Flux.1 Dev FP16 ~23 GB, Wan 2.2 over 15 GB. On a datacenter connection that's minutes, not hours — but budget for it once, then keep weights on Datadrive so every later session skips the download.
- **Big-model training.** Fine-tuning a 70B LLM, or pretraining anything, is multi-GPU, multi-hour territory. One 4090-hour buys LoRA-scale adaptation, not foundation-model work.
- **Your iteration waste.** Nobody keeps every generated image or nails a LoRA config on attempt one. If a third of your outputs are keepers, your effective cost per keeper triples. The same is true on any platform — here the waste is priced in fractions of a cent.
- **This exact price, forever.** $0.49/hour is the verified Glows.ai RTX 4090 rate as of July 2026. If it shifts, every formula in this article still works — swap in the rate you see at checkout.

Even with those caveats, the cheapest mistake in this list costs less than a coffee. That is the real argument for a cheap cloud GPU over a $1,600–$2,000 card purchase: experiments stop needing a business case.
